Beyond Correlation: A Practitioner's Guide to Causal Inference in People Analytics
Suki Wang, PhD  ·  April 2026

There's a question I hear constantly in analytics reviews: "Is this significant?" My follow-up is always the same: "Significant in what sense — statistically, or causally?" These are not the same question. Confusing them has led to some of the most expensive decisions I've seen go sideways — in people analytics, in product analytics, wherever data is supposed to inform action.

Most teams are producing correlations and calling them insights. Employees who attend more meetings have lower engagement. Players who unlock certain achievements churn less. Teams with longer-tenured managers retain more people. These may all be empirically true. None of them tell you what happens if you act on them.

The Fundamental Problem

The core challenge is the fundamental problem of counterfactuals: we can never observe the same unit in both treatment and control simultaneously. We can't see what would have happened to a player if onboarding had been different, or what attrition would look like if the stock hadn't moved. Randomized experiments solve this — random assignment makes groups comparable on everything except treatment. But we can't always run experiments, so we need identification strategies with explicit, defensible assumptions.

The Toolkit

Instrumental Variables (IV) find a variable that shifts treatment but has no direct effect on the outcome. Stock market volatility has served as an instrument for wealth-effect-driven attrition — it shifts financial security without directly determining job satisfaction. Hard part: defending the exclusion restriction.

Difference-in-Differences (DiD) compares outcome changes for treated vs. untreated groups before and after treatment. The key assumption is parallel trends. Staggered DiD with Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators handles varying treatment timing — essential for phased rollouts.

BSTS is my go-to for single-unit interventions hitting everyone at once. It constructs a synthetic counterfactual from control time series, quantifying uncertainty honestly.

"Correlation is not causation" is the beginning of the analysis, not the punchline. The hard work is earning the word "because" with an identification strategy you can defend out loud.

In Practice at Roblox

When we shipped AI meeting features, a pre-post comparison wasn't credible — too many confounds changed simultaneously. We ran randomized eligibility rollouts and used BSTS to validate the aggregate signal. The estimate wasn't the largest number we could have reported — it was the one I could actually defend to a skeptical engineering team. That mattered more.

Good causal analysis isn't about p < 0.05. It's about being honest about what your assumptions require — and then checking whether they hold.

What Survival Models Tell Us About Churn That Classification Simply Can't
Suki Wang, PhD  ·  March 2026

Every analytics team eventually builds a churn model. The default is almost always a binary classifier predicting whether someone leaves within a fixed window. It works. Stakeholders understand it. You can rank users and run a campaign.

But it throws away the most important dimension of the problem: time.

Why Binary Framing Fails

Two players both flagged high-risk by your classifier. One has two sessions played. The other has 200 hours and has been dark for two weeks. Their risk profiles look completely different — not just in magnitude, but in shape. Treating them identically misallocates your intervention budget. Binary classifiers also can't handle censoring — they silently discard all information from users still active at your window's end. Survival models handle censoring correctly by design.

The Core Machinery

The Kaplan-Meier estimator gives you the non-parametric survival curve: probability of remaining past each time point. It's the first thing I plot in any new retention analysis, stratified by a key variable. The visual tells me more than a page of logistic coefficients.

The Cox proportional hazards model extends this to covariates. Each feature shifts the hazard multiplicatively. The proportional hazards assumption is testable with Schoenfeld residuals — and when it fails (which it does in behavioral data), you stratify or use time-varying coefficients.

What I Found at Roblox

Survival models on session-level data revealed that the hazard of drop-off spikes at specific interaction moments — challenge types, loading screens, inventory prompts — and those spikes vary systematically by experience level. A binary outcome metric would never surface this. The survival curve, stratified by experience, made it immediately visible and actionable for Games Engineering.

At Coursera, survival curves stratified by motivation profile showed different hazard shapes, not just different completion levels. Low-motivation learners face a sharp spike at week three that high-motivation learners don't. That shape information was what connected psychometric measurement to product design.

The most useful thing survival analysis gives you isn't better prediction — it's a more honest picture of when risk is concentrated. That's what makes retention interventions actually work.

For competing risks (voluntary vs. involuntary churn), use Fine-Gray subdistribution hazard models. lifelines in Python is excellent. survival in R handles competing risks elegantly. The data engineering — counting process formulation for time-varying covariates — is harder than the modeling, but non-negotiable.

The Projection Problem: Why Your Co-Attendance Network Might Be Lying to You
Suki Wang, PhD  ·  February 2026

Meeting co-attendance networks have become one of the most popular data sources in ONA. If two people attend the same meetings, they're probably interacting, building shared context, influencing each other. Extract those relationships, build a graph, compute centrality — you have a map of the informal organization.

The problem is that this intuition is only partially right. Where it breaks down, it generates systematically misleading findings about peer effects and social contagion. My NetSci 2026 paper is about exactly this.

The Bipartite Projection Problem

Co-attendance data is naturally bipartite: people and meetings, with edges only connecting people to meetings. To get a person-to-person network, you project: two people are connected if they share any meeting. If 500 people attend an all-hands, this creates ~125,000 edges between pairs who may have never spoken. These phantom edges distort every metric you compute.

More dangerously: they create false signals of peer effects. If connected employees show similar engagement scores, the naive interpretation is social influence. But if the edge comes from shared all-hands attendance rather than real interaction, the concordance reflects shared exposure — not contagion.

Concordance Without Contagion

This is the phenomenon I call concordance without contagion. Similar outcomes cluster in the projected network not because of influence, but because projection artifacts make structurally distant people appear adjacent.

The diagnostic tests I develop test whether observed dyadic concordance exceeds what would be expected under a null model that preserves the bipartite structure but destroys peer influence. If concordance is explained by projection density — meeting size, broadcast vs. working group — rather than tie strength controlling for meeting size, that's evidence of artifact, not social influence.

The most common mistake in ONA isn't computing the wrong centrality metric — it's trusting a projection artifact as evidence of social influence. The network looks like a social world, but a broadcast channel can look like a conversation.

What To Do About It

Weight edges by inverse meeting size. A 4-person working group co-attendance carries different information than a 400-person town hall. Classify meeting types and filter large broadcast meetings before building interaction graphs. At Meta, I built network-based inclusion metrics using this filtering logic — separating broadcast from bilateral interaction before making any claim about peer effects or organizational connectivity. Unglamorous, but essential.

Evaluating LLMs for Real Products: What "Good" Actually Means at Scale
Suki Wang, PhD  ·  January 2026

When we shipped Roblox's AI Meeting Intelligence platform, the hardest problem wasn't the model or the integration. It was: how do we know if this is actually good?

Benchmark leaderboards don't answer that. They tell you how a model performs on standardized tasks for users who may be nothing like yours. The moment you put an LLM in a real product, you need evaluation infrastructure specific to your context.

Defining "Good" Precisely

For meeting summaries, we decomposed quality into three distinct dimensions:

  • Factuality: Does the summary accurately represent what was said? Hallucinations in meeting summaries contaminate organizational memory.
  • Usefulness: Does it help the reader act or decide differently? A perfectly accurate summary of a low-stakes meeting may have zero usefulness.
  • Decision impact: Does acting on the output lead to better outcomes? Hardest to measure, most important.

These don't always correlate. Collapsing them into one score destroys the signal that tells you what to fix.

Human-in-the-Loop for Real

A two-stage pipeline: automated signals (confidence scores, embedding similarity, known failure modes) filter out ~75% of outputs that clearly pass or clearly fail before human review. Then structured human evaluation with behavioral anchors for each quality dimension, inter-rater reliability checks, and calibration sessions. The rubric matters enormously — vague "rate overall quality" instructions produce noisy labels.

The goal of human-in-the-loop evaluation is not to replace automated metrics — it's to calibrate them, and to generate labeled data that improves automated signals over time.

The Psychometrics Connection

My PhD background in quantitative psychology turned out to be unexpectedly foundational. Designing a reliable LLM evaluation rubric is exactly the same problem as designing a valid survey instrument: you're measuring a latent construct from noisy, subjective human judgment. The same considerations apply — construct definition, behavioral anchors, rater effects, inter-rater reliability, calibration. My dissertation was about reliable evaluation under noisy human judgment. It was better preparation for LLM eval than anything I could have planned.

LLM evaluation as a field is roughly where psychometrics was in 1980. All the tools to do it rigorously already exist — in a different academic silo. The opportunity for people who can bridge these worlds is significant.
