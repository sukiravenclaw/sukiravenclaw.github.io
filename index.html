    Beyond Correlation: A Practitioner's Guide to Causal Inference in People Analytics
    Suki Wang, PhD · April 2026

    There's a question I hear constantly in analytics reviews: "Is this significant?" My follow-up is always the same: "Significant in what sense — statistically, or causally?" These are not the same question. Confusing them has led to some of the most expensive decisions I've seen go sideways.

    Most teams are producing correlations and calling them insights. Employees who attend more meetings have lower engagement. Players who unlock certain achievements churn less. These may all be empirically true. None of them tell you what happens if you act on them.

    The Fundamental Problem

    The core challenge is the fundamental problem of counterfactuals: we can never observe the same unit in both treatment and control simultaneously. Randomized experiments solve this — but we can't always run them. So we need identification strategies with explicit, defensible assumptions.

    The Toolkit

    Instrumental Variables (IV) find a variable that shifts treatment but has no direct effect on the outcome. Stock market volatility has served as an instrument for wealth-effect-driven attrition — it shifts financial security without directly determining job satisfaction.

    Difference-in-Differences (DiD) compares outcome changes for treated vs. untreated groups before and after treatment. Staggered DiD with Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators handles varying treatment timing — essential for phased rollouts.

    BSTS constructs a synthetic counterfactual from control time series, quantifying uncertainty honestly. My go-to for single-unit interventions hitting everyone at once.

    "Correlation is not causation" is the beginning of the analysis, not the punchline. The hard work is earning the word "because" with an identification strategy you can defend out loud.

    In Practice

    When we shipped AI meeting features at Roblox, a pre-post comparison wasn't credible — too many confounds changed simultaneously. We ran randomized eligibility rollouts and used BSTS to validate the aggregate signal. The estimate we landed on wasn't the largest number we could have reported. But it was the one I could defend — and that mattered more.

    What Survival Models Tell Us About Churn That Classification Simply Can't
    Suki Wang, PhD · March 2026

    Every analytics team eventually builds a churn model. The default is almost always a binary classifier predicting whether someone leaves within a fixed window. It works. Stakeholders understand it.

    But it throws away the most important dimension of the problem: time.

    Why Binary Framing Fails

    Two players flagged as high-risk. One has two sessions played. The other has 200 hours and has been dark for two weeks. Their risk profiles look completely different — not just in magnitude, but in shape. Binary classifiers also can't handle censoring. Survival models handle it correctly by design.

    The Core Machinery

    The Kaplan-Meier estimator gives you the non-parametric survival curve. It's the first thing I plot in any new retention analysis, stratified by a key variable. The visual tells me more than a page of logistic coefficients.

    The Cox proportional hazards model extends this to covariates — each feature shifts the hazard multiplicatively. When the proportional hazards assumption fails, you stratify or use time-varying coefficients.

    What I Found at Roblox

    Survival models revealed that the hazard of drop-off spikes at specific interaction moments — challenge types, loading events, inventory prompts — and those spikes vary by experience level. A binary outcome would never surface this. At Coursera, survival curves stratified by motivation profile showed different hazard shapes, not just levels — connecting psychometric measurement directly to product design.

    The most useful thing survival analysis gives you is a more honest picture of when risk is concentrated. That's what makes retention interventions actually work.

    The Projection Problem: Why Your Co-Attendance Network Might Be Lying to You
    Suki Wang, PhD · February 2026

    Meeting co-attendance networks are powerful. If two people attend the same meetings, they're probably interacting, building shared context, influencing each other. Build a graph, compute centrality — and you have a map of the informal organization. The problem is that this intuition is only partially right.

    The Bipartite Projection Problem

    Co-attendance data is bipartite: people and meetings, edges only connecting people to meetings. To get a person-to-person network, you project it. If 500 people attend an all-hands, this creates ~125,000 edges between pairs who may have never spoken. These phantom edges distort every metric you compute — and create false signals of peer effects.

    Concordance Without Contagion

    If connected employees show similar engagement scores, the naive interpretation is social influence. But if the edge comes from shared all-hands attendance, the concordance reflects shared exposure — not contagion. I call this concordance without contagion. My NetSci 2026 paper develops diagnostic tests to distinguish the two.

    The most common mistake in ONA isn't computing the wrong metric — it's trusting a projection artifact as evidence of social influence.

    What To Do

    Weight edges by inverse meeting size. A 4-person working group co-attendance carries different information than a 400-person town hall. At Meta, I built network-based inclusion metrics using this filtering logic before making any claim about peer effects or organizational connectivity.

    Evaluating LLMs for Real Products: What "Good" Actually Means at Scale
    Suki Wang, PhD · January 2026

    When we shipped Roblox AI Meeting Intelligence, the hardest problem wasn't the model or the integration. It was: how do we know if this is actually good? Benchmark leaderboards don't answer that.

    Defining "Good" Precisely

    For meeting summaries, we decomposed quality into three dimensions: Factuality (does it accurately represent what was said?), Usefulness (does it help the reader act differently?), and Decision impact (does acting on it produce better outcomes?). These don't always correlate. Collapsing them into one score destroys the signal.

    Human-in-the-Loop for Real

    A two-stage pipeline: automated signals filter ~75% of outputs before human review. Then structured human evaluation with behavioral anchors for each quality dimension, inter-rater reliability checks, and calibration sessions. The rubric matters enormously — vague instructions produce noisy labels.

    The goal of human-in-the-loop is not to replace automated metrics — it's to calibrate them, and generate labeled data that improves automated signals over time.

    The Psychometrics Connection

    Designing a reliable LLM evaluation rubric is the same problem as designing a valid survey instrument: measuring a latent construct from noisy, subjective human judgment. My dissertation was about reliable evaluation under noisy human judgment. It was better preparation for LLM eval than anything I could have planned. The LLM evaluation field is about ten years behind where psychometrics was in 1980 — the opportunity for people who can bridge these worlds is significant.
